Here are some of the most prevalent attack vectors that criminals use to compromise systems through password-related vulnerabilities:

1. **Phishing**: Attackers often use deceptive emails and websites to trick users into revealing their passwords. This social engineering tactic exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

2. **Brute Force Attacks**: In brute force attacks, attackers systematically try every possible combination of characters until they find the correct password. While time-consuming against strong passwords, weak passwords can be cracked relatively quickly.

3. **Credential Stuffing**: This method involves using leaked username and password combinations from one service to gain unauthorized access to accounts on different services, taking advantage of users' tendency to reuse passwords.

4. **Password Spraying**: Unlike brute force, which targets a single account with many passwords, password spraying involves using a few common passwords against many accounts. This can help evade detection by not triggering account lockouts.

5. **Keylogging**: Malicious software or hardware is used to record keystrokes on a user's device, capturing passwords and other sensitive information as users type them.

6. **Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attacks**: Here, attackers intercept communication between users and websites to capture login credentials. This can occur over insecure networks, such as public Wi-Fi, when encryption is not properly implemented.

7. **Rainbow Table Attacks**: These involve the use of precomputed lists of hashed password values to crack hashed passwords. If password hashes are stored without sufficient complexity, attackers can reverse-engineer the original passwords using rainbow tables.

8. **Social Engineering**: Beyond phishing, social engineering exploits trust and psychological manipulation to trick individuals into revealing their passwords. Examples include pretexting, baiting, and impersonation.

9. **Exploiting Default and Weak Passwords**: Many systems and devices, particularly IoT devices, come with default credentials that users often fail to change, making them easy targets for attackers.

10. **Dictionary Attacks**: Similar to brute force but more refined, dictionary attacks use lists of common passwords and variations to steal credentials. They exploit users' tendency to choose simple and predictable passwords.

To mitigate these risks, organizations and individuals should adopt robust password policies that include using complex, unique passwords for each account.
